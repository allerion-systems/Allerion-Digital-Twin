## 1 · Win the job

**1.1 — Turn a rough description into a clean estimate**
> You're my estimator. Write a clear, itemized estimate for this job. Use friendly but professional language a homeowner understands. Job: `[DESCRIBE: e.g. tear off and replace 22sq architectural shingle roof, 2 layers, new underlayment, 4 pipe boots]`. My pricing notes: `[YOUR PRICE BOOK / RULES]`. Output: scope of work, line items with quantities, a total range, and 2 optional upgrades I can upsell.

**1.2 — Write the proposal that closes**
> Turn this estimate into a one-page proposal that makes us look like the obvious choice. Lead with the outcome for the homeowner, then scope, then price, then why us. Confident, warm, no jargon. Estimate: `[PASTE 1.1 OUTPUT]`. About us: `[1-2 LINES ABOUT YOUR COMPANY]`.

**1.3 — Handle the "your price is too high" objection**
> A homeowner says our quote is higher than a competitor's. Write a short, confident text reply that re-frames on value (warranty, crew, materials, no surprises) without trashing the other guy or dropping price. Keep it under 4 sentences.

**1.4 — The follow-up that un-sticks a stalled quote**
> Write 3 follow-up texts for a homeowner who got our quote `[N]` days ago and went quiet. #1 helpful nudge, #2 add a reason to act (season/availability), #3 the polite "should I close your file?" Each under 3 sentences, friendly, no desperation.

**1.5 — Storm/insurance claim explainer**
> Write a simple text explaining how we help with an insurance claim for storm damage, what the homeowner needs to do, and that we handle the adjuster meeting. Reassuring, plain English, under 5 sentences.

## 2 · Never lose a lead

**2.1 — Missed-call text-back script** (the one that books jobs)
> Write a friendly auto-text to send when we miss a call. It should: apologize for missing them, ask if it's a repair or full replacement, and offer to book a free inspection. Sound like a local owner, not a robot. Under 2 sentences. Then write 2 follow-up questions to qualify the job (insurance? timeline?).

**2.2 — Speed-to-lead reply for web/Facebook/Angi leads**
> A new lead just came in from `[SOURCE]` with this message: `[PASTE LEAD]`. Write a reply I can send in under 60 seconds that answers their question, builds trust, and asks for the appointment. Warm, specific, no copy-paste feel.

**2.3 — Reactivate a dead lead**
> Write a text to re-engage a homeowner who asked about `[JOB]` `[N]` months ago and never moved forward. Give them a low-pressure reason to talk now. Under 3 sentences.

**2.4 — After-hours responder**
> Write a short auto-reply for texts/messages that come in after hours: acknowledge them, set expectations on when we'll call, and capture the basics (name, address, what they need).

## 3 · Get paid faster

**3.1 — The invoice reminder that doesn't sound mean**
> Write a friendly payment reminder text for an invoice that's `[N]` days overdue. Assume the best, make it easy to pay, give a simple next step. Under 3 sentences. Then write a firmer (but still polite) version for `[N+14]` days.

**3.2 — Deposit request**
> Write a clear, professional message requesting a `[%]` deposit to schedule the job, explaining what it covers and how to pay. Reassuring, not pushy.

**3.3 — Change-order, in writing**
> The homeowner asked for `[CHANGE]` mid-job. Write a short, friendly message that documents the change, the added cost, and asks them to confirm in writing before we proceed.

## 4 · Reviews & referrals

**4.1 — The review request that actually works**
> Write a text to send the day after we finish a job asking for a Google review. Make it personal, mention the specific work, and include a one-tap ask. Under 3 sentences.

**4.2 — Turn a happy customer into referrals**
> Write a message to a happy past customer asking if they know a neighbor who needs `[SERVICE]`, with a simple referral thank-you offer.

**4.3 — Respond to a bad review**
> Write a calm, professional public reply to this negative review that takes the high road, shows we care, and moves it offline: `[PASTE REVIEW]`.

## 5 · Hire & run the crew

**5.1 — Job posting that gets applicants**
> Write a job post for a `[ROLE]` at my `[TRADE]` company in `[CITY]`. Sell why it's a good place to work, list must-haves, and end with a simple way to apply. No corporate fluff.

**5.2 — Screen applicants fast**
> Write 6 short screening questions (text or phone) to quickly tell if a `[ROLE]` applicant is worth an interview.

**5.3 — Turn a process into an SOP**
> I'm going to describe how we do `[TASK: e.g. close out a job]`. Turn it into a clean, numbered SOP a new hire could follow, with a checklist at the end. Here's how we do it: `[DESCRIBE]`.

## 6 · Marketing that fits in your truck

**6.1 — Facebook post that gets calls**
> Write a Facebook post for my `[TRADE]` company about `[TOPIC: e.g. fall roof check-ups]`. Local, friendly, one clear call to action. Include 3 hashtags and a line for the comments.

**6.2 — Google Business Profile post**
> Write a short Google Business Profile update promoting `[OFFER/SERVICE]` with a call-to-action button suggestion.

**6.3 — Door-hanger / flyer copy**
> Write punchy door-hanger copy for after we finish a job in a neighborhood: "we just `[did X]` for your neighbor," a reason to call us, and our offer.

**6.4 — Simple email to past customers**
> Write a short seasonal email to past customers offering `[SERVICE/CHECK-UP]`. Warm, helpful, one CTA.

## 7 · Office & admin

**7.1 — Supplier/PO email**
> Write a clear email to `[SUPPLIER]` ordering `[MATERIALS]` for the `[JOB]`, asking for confirmation of price and delivery date.

**7.2 — Schedule the week**
> Here are my jobs and crews for the week: `[PASTE LIST]`. Suggest an efficient schedule that minimizes driving, flags conflicts, and leaves buffer for weather.

**7.3 — "Ask your business" (once your data's in)**
> Based on these jobs/notes, answer: `[QUESTION: e.g. which job type makes us the most profit per day?]`. Data: `[PASTE]`.
